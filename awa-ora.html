Awa Ora - Mātauranga Māori River Restoration App

A Te Ao Māori–inspired app that supports a community garden dedicated to restoring Te Taiao (the natural world) and revitalizing local Awa (rivers). The app empowers users to connect through mātauranga Māori (traditional knowledge), participate in hands-on workshops, and contribute to environmental action. Designed as a digital gateway to real-world impact, it helps communities learn, collaborate, and nurture both land and people.

My Role

Team Leader, Project Manager.

Brand Identity, UX Design, UX Research, UI Designer, Copywriting

Our colour palette went through a thoughtful process initially taking on a deep pounamu green palette that would emphasise on the moss and native living organisms within local awa. The decision to shift towards hues of blue, indigo and purple arised when the green hues were creating a salt-water representation rather than fresh-water rivers.

Our final colour palette as seen above symbolises clarity, spirituality, and mystical properties of freshwater rivers. Our gradient mimics the visualisation of light shining through water, a concept in te ao māori reinforced by 'wiri' the shimmering and moving energy found in nature.

As our research evolved, so did our design philosophy. We re-centered the project on the health of Aotearoa’s rivers, recognising the awa as both a living entity and a symbol of interconnected wellbeing. This led us to co-design a space rooted in kaitiakitanga, with a digital companion—the Awa Ora app—serving as a tool for citizen science and cultural reconnection. Our visual identity shifted from pounamu greens to a gradient of blues and purples, capturing the spiritual essence of freshwater and its healing qualities in Te Ao Māori. The interface was intentionally minimal, inspired by the concept of a taonga—a treasured object—allowing users to engage meaningfully with the river, their community, and the environment through the pillars of the Home, Community, and Te Taiao tabs.

Te Taiao Framework as Foundation We adopted the Te Taiao framework to guide design—recognising the environment, people, and culture as a single interconnected system. Initially we planned on breaking it down between animals, insects soil, water and plants. Te Taiao framework inspired us to create the app through a te ao māori lens, focusing on the four elements of te taiao; wai, whenua, hau and ahi. This framework ended up feeling more accurate and translated the information in a more meaningful way for users to digest.

The app was designed to feel like a taonga, a sacred tool—minimal, intuitive, and responsive to the river’s health.

Three Pillar Structure The app centres on: Home (real-time awa health) Community (local updates + events) Te Taiao (education through Māori environmental values) aims to create an accessible, attractive interface for all ages, funneling interaction into memorable and user-friendly navigation, and heavy blocks of crucial information into digestable text.

Design Decisions

1. Shift from “Smart” Garden to Community-Led Space Initial concepts focused on automation and technology. Research revealed this could harm community connection. We pivoted to a low-tech, people-first approach grounded in tikanga and kaitiakitanga.

2. Focus on River Health Food security and pesticide issues led us to a deeper theme: the declining health of Aotearoa’s rivers. The project reframed the garden as a tool for restoring the mauri of the awa.

3. Checking in with local whakairo I spoke with whanau from my iwi near Wairoa River to learn purakai (stories) and used patterns observed from my marae when working on Awa Ora.

4. Colour Palette Redefined Originally pounamu green, we shifted to blues and purples—symbolising clarity, spirituality, and the life force of freshwater.

5. App as a Digital Taonga The app was designed to feel like a sacred tool—minimal, intuitive, and responsive to the river’s health.

Challenges

One of the main challenges was navigating the balance between technological innovation and authentic community engagement. Our initial direction leaned heavily on automation and emerging tech, but deeper research into Aotearoa’s local communities revealed a risk: that a high-tech solution could unintentionally alienate the very people we were trying to connect with. We also faced conceptual challenges in defining what a “futuristic” garden meant within a Te Ao Māori context—how to honour traditional knowledge systems while still responding to modern environmental concerns. Interpreting and applying frameworks like Te Taiao with cultural integrity required constant consultation, reflection, and adaptation. This was not a linear process, and it tested our assumptions about design, sustainability, and co-creation.
